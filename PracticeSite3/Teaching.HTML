Who am I as a Teacher?

As an educator, I view my role as more than teaching English literature; I strive to help students develop their confidence, critical thinking skills, and self-awareness necessary to navigate the world beyond the classroom. I am currently entering my third year of teaching. While academic growth is important, I believe meaningful learning occurs when students feel supported, valued, and the lessons are capable of access which leads to student success.

I work to create an environment where students feel comfortable taking intellectual risks, asking questions, and learning from mistakes. Through positive reinforcement and relationship-building, I aim to foster a classroom culture in which students recognize their own strengths and develop confidence in their abilities. When students believe their voices matter, they are more willing to engage deeply with learning and challenge themselves to grow.

Teaching Philosophy

As an educator these are a few quotes that I live by:

"Actually, it's more like we're looking for a needle in a pile of needles. A needle would stand out in a haystack"

-Dr. Spencer Reid, "Criminal Minds" (Season 1, Episode 4)

It is a little odd to follow a quote from a television show about analyzing criminals and their behaviors, but this also rings true for education. Think about it this way - it reflects a common challenge within education. Students are often presented with large amounts of information that seem equally important, making it difficult to identify the specific detail, theme, or piece of evidence that matters most. The challenge is not finding something that is obviously different but being able to distinguish one important idea from many similar ones.

"It is important to draw wisdom from many different places. If we take it from only one place, it becomes rigid and stale."

-Uncle Iroh, "Avatar the Last Airbender" (Book 2: Earth, Episode 9)

While this is also fictional, it provides a lesson to myself (as a teacher) and to my students who are tasked with learning from me. I believe education is most effective when students are exposed to a variety of perspectives, voices, and experiences. This can be achieved through literature, discussion, and reflection, students learn not only academic skills but also empathy, critical thinking, and a deeper understanding of themselves and others. As a teacher, my role in the students lives is not to teach them English literature but to prepare them for the world around them.

Because every student enters the classroom with unique experiences, strengths, and challenges, I strive to meet students where they are and support their growth from that point forward. After all, learning is not a one-size-fits-all process, and meaningful growth only occurs when students feel valued, challenged, and supported.
